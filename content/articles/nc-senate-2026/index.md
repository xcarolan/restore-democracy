 ## The NC Senate Race That Could Decide Everything: Cooper vs. Whatley {.text-center}
---

## Introduction

North Carolina has a habit of deciding things. The state has picked the winning presidential candidate in 13 of the last 14 elections. Its Senate races regularly determine which party controls the chamber. And in 2026, the Tar Heel State is hosting what may be the most consequential Senate race in the country.

On one side: Roy Cooper, a Democrat who served 40 years in public office, expanded Medicaid to 600,000 North Carolinians, and just finished two terms as governor. On the other: Michael Whatley, the former RNC Chair who helped Trump win the state twice and built a reputation as one of the GOP's most effective party operators.

This site evaluates candidates against two questions: Do they respect the Constitution and take their oath of office seriously? And do they genuinely represent and serve the people who elected them? I've tried to apply both questions equally here. You can judge whether I succeeded.

---

## The Candidates

### Roy Cooper: The Career Public Servant
{{< figure src="Roy_Cooper.jpg" title="Roy Cooper" width="600" >}}   

Cooper's resume is long. State House at 28. State Senate Majority Leader. Four terms as Attorney General. Two terms as Governor. He worked with Republicans to grant the governor veto power, then used that veto 104 times — nearly triple all other NC governors combined since 1997.

His record has real achievements. Medicaid expansion brought healthcare to over 600,000 low-income North Carolinians, accomplished by persuading a Republican-controlled legislature to cooperate. Teacher pay rose 19% over eight years. North Carolina won "best state for business" awards three of the past four years. He signed legislation putting utilities on a path to carbon neutrality and repealed the controversial HB2 "bathroom bill."

But Cooper's record also has genuine failures that deserve equal scrutiny.

His disaster response after Hurricane Helene drew significant criticism — serious enough that his successor dismantled the emergency management office Cooper had built and replaced it entirely. That's not a minor footnote. When constituents needed their government most, the system failed, and Cooper bears responsibility for how that office was structured and run.

The legislature overrode 52 of his vetoes. Cooper's supporters frame this as Republican obstruction; his critics frame it as executive overreach. The truth is probably somewhere in between, but 52 overrides suggests a governor who sometimes pushed harder than the democratic process supported.

His immigration veto — blocking requirements for sheriffs to cooperate with federal immigration agents — remains genuinely controversial. Supporters argue it protected due process and community trust in local law enforcement. Critics argue it prioritized a political position over public safety. Reasonable people disagree, and it's worth naming that honestly.

Cooper's through-line is institutional. He has consistently worked within systems, accepted democratic outcomes even when they went against him, and kept his personal financial interests largely separate from his public role.

### Michael Whatley: The Party Strategist
{{< figure src="Michael_Whatley.jpg" title="Michael Whatley" width="600" >}}   

Whatley's background is fundamentally different. He has never held elected office. He has no legislative voting record. What he does have is 17 years as an energy lobbyist for oil, gas, and electric utilities, followed by a successful career as a party organizer and fundraiser.

As NC GOP Chair from 2019–2024, Whatley helped deliver the state for Trump twice. As RNC Chair in 2024, he oversaw significant Republican gains nationally. His organizational and fundraising skills are not in question — he's demonstrably good at winning elections.

But there are legitimate questions his campaign hasn't fully answered. Seventeen years lobbying for energy companies creates real conflict-of-interest concerns for a senator who would vote on energy policy. His personal investments in energy stocks reportedly increased after Trump administration policy decisions. This isn't illegal, but voters deserve a clear accounting of how he would handle those conflicts in office.

More seriously, after the 2020 election, Whatley echoed claims about widespread fraud that courts and election officials in multiple states — including Republican-led states — found no credible evidence to support. He participated in the Georgia call where pressure was applied to officials to revisit certified results. When asked about January 6th, he placed responsibility solely on the rioters, declining to address the role of the pressure campaign that preceded it.

These are facts. What voters make of them is their call.

What's harder to evaluate is Whatley's governing philosophy, because he hasn't governed. We don't know how he'd vote on legislation that pits constituent interests against party interests. We don't know how he'd handle a genuine constitutional conflict. His record shows he's effective at winning power. How he'd exercise it remains an open question.

## The Broader Context: Party, Power, and North Carolina's Democratic Institutions

### The Burr Censure

In February 2021, Republican Senator Richard Burr voted to convict Donald Trump in his second impeachment trial, citing his belief that Trump bore responsibility for the events of January 6th. It was a vote that cost Burr politically — but it was a vote grounded in his reading of his constitutional oath.

The North Carolina Republican Party, under Whatley's leadership, responded by censuring Burr.

This is worth sitting with. A sitting senator made a constitutional judgment that conflicted with party preference. The state party chairman's response was punishment, not debate. Whatever you think of Burr's vote, the censure sent a clear message about what the NC GOP under Whatley expected from its elected officials: party loyalty over independent constitutional judgment.

That's directly relevant to what kind of senator Whatley would be.

### The Trump Endorsement

Trump has endorsed Whatley, and it's worth being clear about what that means — and what it doesn't.

In North Carolina, Trump's endorsement is politically powerful. He won the state twice. His support base is real, motivated, and loyal. For a significant portion of NC voters, the endorsement is a genuine positive, and dismissing it as simply disqualifying would be both condescending and inaccurate.

But endorsements come with context. Trump's endorsement of Whatley follows a period in which Whatley echoed his fraud claims, participated in pressure on certified election results, and led the censure of a senator who broke with him on constitutional grounds. The endorsement isn't incidental to that record — it's connected to it.

The question voters have to answer isn't whether Trump's endorsement is good or bad. It's whether a candidate with that history and those alliances would be able to exercise independent constitutional judgment when it conflicts with what the party — and the president — want.

The Burr censure suggests that under Whatley's leadership, the answer to that question was no.

### North Carolina's Gerrymandering Record

There is a third piece of context that belongs here, and it requires the same honest treatment.

North Carolina's legislative maps have been struck down by courts multiple times — for racial gerrymandering, for unconstitutional partisan gerrymandering, and for violating voters' rights. This isn't political opinion. It's documented legal record, with rulings from both federal and state courts — including *NAACP v. McCrory* (4th Cir. 2016), *Common Cause v. Lewis* (2019), and *Harper v. Hall* (NC Supreme Court, 2022). Full citations are in the Sources section.

Gerrymandering, to be clear, is not exclusively a Republican sin. Democrats have drawn self-serving maps in Maryland, Illinois, and elsewhere. It is a structural disease in American democracy that both parties have exploited when they had the power to do so.

But in North Carolina, it has been the Republican-controlled legislature drawing maps that courts have repeatedly ruled went too far — maps drawn during the period when Whatley led the state party. The through-line is the same as the Burr censure: a pattern of using institutional power to entrench partisan advantage rather than serve the broader electorate.

Your second criterion asks whether a candidate will genuinely represent their constituents. Gerrymandering is, at its core, the practice of choosing your voters rather than letting voters choose their representatives. It is one of the most direct ways that constituent representation gets hollowed out from the inside.

Whatley didn't draw the maps. But he led the party that did, during the period when courts found those maps unconstitutional. That's a relevant fact for voters applying this criterion.

### Taken Together

The Burr censure, the Trump endorsement, and the gerrymandering record aren't separate issues. They form a pattern. Under Whatley's leadership, the NC GOP consistently chose partisan entrenchment over democratic openness — punishing constitutional independence, backing claims that undermined election integrity, and drawing maps courts found went beyond legal bounds.

Cooper is not without his own institutional failures. But his record doesn't show the same pattern of treating democratic structures as instruments of partisan advantage.

Voters can weigh that pattern however they choose. But they should weigh it.

## Applying the Two Criteria

### Does this candidate respect the Constitution and take their oath seriously?

Cooper's record here is strong. His handling of the Duke Lacrosse case — taking over from a local DA, dropping charges, and declaring the players innocent despite political pressure — is a concrete example of someone choosing institutional integrity over political convenience. His veto use was aggressive but constitutional. He left office gracefully.

Whatley's record raises legitimate concerns. Promoting claims of widespread election fraud that lacked evidentiary support, and participating in pressure on certified election results, are in tension with the constitutional principle that elections are decided by voters and certified through established legal processes. Whether those actions reflect a governing philosophy or the pressures of a specific political moment is something voters will have to weigh.

### Does this candidate genuinely represent and serve their constituents?

Cooper's 40-year public record gives voters a lot to evaluate. Medicaid expansion and teacher pay increases are concrete constituent benefits. The Hurricane Helene failures are concrete constituent harms. His immigration veto is a genuine policy disagreement about what "serving constituents" means when different constituents want different things.

Whatley's constituent record doesn't exist yet in a governing sense. His lobbying background raises the question of which constituents — energy industry clients or North Carolina voters — would have his first loyalty. That's a fair question to ask, and his campaign's answer to it matters.

---

## Why This Race Matters

This seat could determine Senate control, with real consequences for judicial appointments, legislative agendas, and executive oversight. That's the partisan math, and it's real.

But beyond the math, this race is a test of what voters value in a senator. Governing experience versus political effectiveness. Institutional record versus party loyalty. Known failures versus unknown capabilities.

Cooper's failures are documented. Whatley's potential failures are speculative — which cuts both ways. You know what you're getting with Cooper, including the parts that didn't work. With Whatley, you're making a bet.

---

## Conclusion

Roy Cooper offers four decades of public service, a documented record of both achievements and failures, and a consistent pattern of respecting democratic institutions even when they constrained him.

Michael Whatley offers political effectiveness, party loyalty, and a set of open questions — about his relationship to democratic norms, his energy industry ties, and how he'd govern when party interest conflicts with constituent interest.

Applying this site's two criteria as evenhandedly as I can: Cooper's record is stronger on institutional respect and constituent representation. Whatley's record raises questions that, in my view, he hasn't fully answered.

But I've tried to show my work here. If you see it differently, I'd genuinely like to know why.

### North Carolina Gerrymandering — Court Cases

- **NC State Conference of the NAACP v. McCrory**, 831 F.3d 204 (4th Cir. 2016) — Federal appeals court struck down NC congressional districts as unconstitutional racial gerrymanders.

- **Common Cause v. Lewis**, No. 18-CVS-014001 (Wake Co. Super. Ct., Sept. 3, 2019) — NC state court struck down 2017 legislative maps as an unconstitutional partisan gerrymander under the NC Constitution.

- **Rucho v. Common Cause**, 588 U.S. 684 (2019) — U.S. Supreme Court ruled federal courts cannot adjudicate partisan gerrymandering claims, but explicitly left open state constitutional challenges. The case arose from NC's congressional map.

- **Harper v. Hall (Harper I)**, 380 N.C. 317 (2022) — NC Supreme Court (then with a Democratic majority) struck down 2021 legislative and congressional maps as unconstitutional partisan gerrymanders under the NC Constitution.

- **Harper v. Hall (Harper III)**, 384 N.C. 292 (2023) — NC Supreme Court (now with a Republican majority, following the 2022 midterms) reversed Harper I, ruling that partisan gerrymandering claims are non-justiciable under the NC Constitution. The court's reversal of its own four-month-old ruling drew national attention.

- **Moore v. Harper**, 600 U.S. 1 (2023) — U.S. Supreme Court rejected the "independent state legislature" theory that NC Republican legislators had advanced, reaffirming that state courts retain authority to review election laws under state constitutions.

- **NC NAACP v. NC General Assembly** (ongoing, 2023–2025) — Federal trial began June 2025 in Winston-Salem challenging 2023 congressional and legislative maps as racial gerrymanders violating the Voting Rights Act.
